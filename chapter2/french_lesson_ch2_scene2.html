💡 faire + infinitif (the causative) is essential. It means "to have/make someone do something," or to cause an action. Il les fit entrer = He had them enter (showed them in). Je fais réparer ma voiture = I'm having my car repaired. Elle me fait rire = She makes me laugh. Notice how les (direct object pronoun = "them") sits before the verb — this is how French handles "him/her/it/them" when they're objects: je le vois (I see him/it), elle les connaît (she knows them).

💡 Two key patterns here. First, dit-il / dit-elle — French inverts the subject and verb in dialogue tags, always with a hyphen: dit-il (he said), demanda-t-elle (she asked — note the -t- buffer when the verb ends in a vowel and the pronoun begins with one). Second, d'une voix + adjectif is the standard French way to describe how something is said: d'une voix tremblante (in a trembling voice), d'une voix basse (in a low voice), d'une voix claire (in a clear voice). 💡 se laisser + infinitif = to let oneself (do something) — a common and expressive structure. se laisser tomber (to sink / drop into), se laisser aller (to let oneself go), se laisser convaincre (to let oneself be convinced). The subject is passive in intent but still does the action. Also note the superlative: le plus + adjectif = "the most …" — le plus proche (the nearest), la plus belle (the most beautiful), les plus fort(e)s (the strongest). The article (le/la/les) agrees with the noun. Finally, les mains tremblantes is another body-language set phrase — same pattern as les bras croisés from Scene 1.
