💡 avoir l'air + adjectif is how French says "to look / seem." Tu as l'air fatigué = You look tired. Elle a l'air heureuse = She seems happy. The adjective agrees with the subject being described, not with air itself. Also note the comparative scaffolding: beaucoup plus + adjectif + que = much more [adj] than — and que contracts to qu' before a vowel (qu'avant).

💡 Two written-French conventions stacked in one line. dit-il / dit-elle flips verb and subject after a quoted line — a pure writing convention, never in speech. With verbs ending in a vowel, a -t- buffer is inserted: demanda-t-elle. Pair it with d'une voix + adjectif, the standard way to describe how someone speaks: d'une voix basse, d'une voix tremblante, d'une voix ferme.
